01

Investment Summary

We initiate coverage of JPMorgan Chase (JPM) with an Overweight rating and a $350 price target, implying ~4.5% upside plus a ~1.8% dividend yield. JPM remains the highest-quality large-cap bank franchise in the U.S., posting a 23% ROTCE in Q1 2026 — well above peers — while trading at a valuation that only partially reflects that gap.

JPMorgan delivered Q1 2026 net income of $16.5 billion and diluted EPS of $5.94, up 13% and 17% year-over-year respectively, beating consensus EPS estimates by roughly 9%. Revenue grew 10% to $50.5 billion (managed basis) as strength in Markets, investment banking fees, and asset management offset a moderating net interest income outlook. Management trimmed full-year 2026 NII guidance slightly to ~$103 billion, which triggered a short-term pullback in the stock despite the headline beat — a "beat and retreat" pattern common in bank earnings reactions.

The franchise's scale (​$4.9 trillion in assets, $2.7 trillion in deposits) and diversification across consumer banking, markets, investment banking, and asset management give it more levers to pull than mono-line peers when any single business slows. Capital return remains robust: $4.1 billion in dividends and $8.3 billion of net buybacks in the quarter alone, supported by a 14.3% CET1 ratio comfortably above regulatory minimums.

Why Overweight

  • ROTCE of 23% is the highest among large-cap U.S. banks, justifying a premium multiple
  • Diversified revenue mix cushions against any single-segment slowdown
  • Fortress balance sheet (14.3% CET1) supports continued buybacks and dividend growth
  • Valuation has not fully re-rated to reflect the ROTCE premium versus peers

03

Industry & Competitive Positioning

Large-cap U.S. banks are navigating a "neutral-for-longer" rate environment: not the tailwind of rapid hikes, nor the relief of aggressive cuts. Loan growth is steady but unspectacular, credit costs are normalizing off recent lows, and Basel III capital-rule finalization remains an overhang on the group's capital planning. Against this backdrop, franchise quality and cost discipline separate the winners.

04

Investment Thesis

1

Best-in-class returns on capital fund a self-reinforcing buyback cycle

At 23% ROTCE, JPMorgan generates capital faster than it can profitably redeploy into loan growth alone, which is why buybacks ($8.3B in Q1 2026 alone) have become a structural part of the capital return story. Continued buybacks reduce share count and support EPS growth even if net income growth moderates.

2

Diversification dampens the "one bad segment" risk that hits mono-line peers

When NII growth slows, as guided for 2026, Markets and investment banking fee income (up double digits in Q1 2026) can offset it. Peers more concentrated in traditional lending don't have the same offset.

3

Fortress balance sheet provides optionality, not just safety

A 14.3% CET1 ratio is well above requirements, giving management room to lean into opportunities — such as the Apple Card portfolio acquisition and data-center lending — that smaller or less-capitalized banks must pass on.

Valuation

Banks are valued primarily on P/E and Price-to-Tangible-Book-Value (P/TBV) rather than a standard DCF, since a bank's balance sheet and cash flows don't map cleanly onto free-cash-flow models. We triangulate using both.

Method 1 — Price / Earnings

Applying a 13.5x–14.5x multiple (near the upper end of JPM's historical range, reflecting its ROTCE premium) to 2027E EPS of $25.50:

13.5x × $25.50 = $344  |  14.5x × $25.50 = $370

Method 2 — Price / Tangible Book Value

Growing current TBVPS of $108.87 at ~8%/yr to a 2027E TBVPS of ~$127, and applying a 2.6x–2.8x target multiple (consistent with JPM's premium ROTCE):

2.6x × $127 = $330  |  2.8x × $127 = $356

Key Risks

Regulatory

Basel III "endgame" capital rules remain unresolved; a stricter final version would raise capital requirements and could pressure buyback capacity.

Rate path

Full-year NII guidance was already trimmed once in 2026; further Fed cuts beyond expectations would pressure net interest income more than modeled.

Credit cycle

Card net charge-off rates are guided toward ~3.4% for 2026; a weaker labor market would push consumer credit costs higher than assumed here.

Succession

CEO Jamie Dimon's eventual succession is an active, closely watched process; leadership transition risk is real for a franchise this closely associated with one leader.

Competition

Private credit funds and non-bank lenders continue to take share in parts of commercial lending, a dynamic management has flagged directly.
